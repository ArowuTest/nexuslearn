Teaching Journey

warm_up

motion-effect-compare

For each example, identify the movement change: start, stop, speed up, slow down or change direction.

Activate observable force effects before naming specific forces.

Audio: A force is inferred from what changes in an object's motion or shape.

Visual: Before-and-after motion cards show direction and speed without requiring animation.

concept_launch

force-arrow-model-build

Use arrows to show which forces act and which way they act.

Introduce qualitative force-arrow models as explanations, not decorative labels.

Audio: Arrow direction shows the direction of the force. Compare arrows only within the model's stated scale.

Visual: An object sits between labelled motion and force-arrow layers with a model-not-to-scale note.

worked_example

parachute-fair-test

Two equal masses fall with different canopy areas. The larger canopy produces more air resistance and falls more slowly.

Model a controlled comparison and explain an outcome using gravity and air resistance.

Audio: Mass and drop height stay the same. Canopy area changes. The larger area increases air resistance.

Visual: Side-by-side parachutes share mass and height labels, while area and fall-time data differ.

guided_practice

variable-data-force-sync

Choose one variable, predict the effect, run repeated trials and explain the pattern in the data.

Coordinate fair-test planning, simulation evidence and causal explanation.

Audio: Change one factor, keep the comparison fair and use repeated results rather than one dramatic trial.

Visual: A simulator links CHANGE-MEASURE-KEEP SAME controls to a data table and force model.

checkpoint

same-mass-shape-compare

A flat paper sheet falls more slowly than the same sheet crumpled. Does this prove the crumpled paper has more gravity?

Distinguish gravity from differing air resistance and challenge heavier-falls-faster reasoning.

Audio: The mass is the same. Shape changes the air resistance, so the falling motion changes.

Visual: Equal-mass labels remain fixed while area and resistance arrows change.

teach_back

lever-tradeoff-replay

Teach your companion how a lever can reduce the input force without giving free work.

Explain force multiplication alongside the increased distance moved and pivot position.

Audio: A smaller force acts over a greater distance. Show the input, pivot, load and movement trade-off.

Visual: A lever model compares two pivot positions with input force and distance readouts.

Question Variants

sc-y5-forces-q-paper-fall

force-simulator review difficulty 6

The same sheet of paper is dropped flat and then crumpled from the same height. Why does the crumpled paper usually reach the ground first?

Both forms are pulled towards Earth by gravity, but the flat sheet has a larger area and experiences more air resistance, so it falls more slowly.

sc-y5-forces-q-parachute-plan

fair-test-plan review difficulty 6

Plan a fair test of how parachute canopy area affects fall time.

A fair comparison changes canopy area, measures fall time and keeps mass, drop height and material the same across repeated trials.

sc-y5-forces-q-friction-grip

evidence-explain review difficulty 5

A shoe with a textured sole slips less than a smooth sole on the same dry surface. Which explanation best fits?

The evidence suggests greater friction between the textured sole and the dry surface, providing more grip and resisting sliding.

sc-y5-forces-q-lever-tradeoff

mechanism-model review difficulty 8

A learner moves a lever's pivot closer to the load. What is the likely effect at the long input end?

Placing the pivot closer to the load gives the input side a longer lever arm, so a smaller input force can work, but the input end must move through a greater distance.

gravity-and-falling-models

force-simulator developing 300 planned

Explain falling towards Earth and distinguish gravity effects from air-resistance differences.

Avoid claims about ideal vacuum behaviour beyond the evidence shown; distinguish mass from weight and do not imply gravity is absent when an object is supported.

air-and-water-resistance-tests

fair-test-plan expected 320 planned

Plan and interpret controlled comparisons of shape, area or speed in air and water.

Virtual contexts must be safe and physically plausible; conclusions should match the tested range rather than claim universal rules.

friction-grip-and-heating

evidence-explain expected 280 planned

Explain friction as an opposing contact force that can slow movement, provide grip or cause heating.

Use age-appropriate everyday examples and avoid unsafe instructions involving burns, roads or uncontrolled sliding.

Reason qualitatively about how mechanisms change force, direction, speed or distance.

Do not present exact mechanical advantage unless the model supplies sufficient quantities; always expose the trade-off or changed direction.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm virtual lab with muted backgrounds, no collision sounds, no rapid drops and one active force model at a time.

reduced_motion

Replace falling, flowing and rotating animations with numbered still frames, direction arrows and complete data tables.

audio_first

Announce the object, motion, changed variable, measured result and force explanation in that order, with replay at every evidence point.

reading_support

Use short investigation cards, icons for change/measure/keep same, glossary popovers and force-arrow descriptions that do not require interpreting dense diagrams.

attention_support

Lock irrelevant controls, display PREDICT-TEST-RECORD-EXPLAIN progress and pause automatically after each trial for observation.

processing_support

Remove timers, provide quarter-speed and no-motion modes, preserve trial data, allow repeated runs and expose graph values in a sortable table.

confidence_support

Treat predictions as testable ideas, preserve correct variable choices during repair and reward updating an explanation when evidence changes.

eal_support

Preteach force, gravity, resistance, friction, surface, variable, input and output with gestures, paired arrows and sentence frames.
