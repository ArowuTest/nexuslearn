Teaching Journey

warm_up

context-sort-columns

Sort each word card by whether it is used in an everyday way, a technical subject way, or both.

Reactivate flexible word meaning and expose the everyday-versus-disciplinary trap.

Audio: Some words change meaning depending on the subject and sentence around them.

Visual: Word cards move between everyday, technical and both columns with example-sentence previews.

concept_launch

morpheme-tile-split

Break the word into prefix, root and suffix, then predict its meaning and word class.

Introduce morphology as a reusable strategy for unfamiliar academic words.

Audio: Prefixes, roots and suffixes are clues. They do not replace context, but they give us a strong first prediction.

Visual: A word splits into labelled morpheme tiles that feed a meaning prediction card.

worked_example

discipline-lens-focus

In science, why does 'observe' mean more than just 'look'?

Model subject-context reasoning and precise definition building.

Audio: In science, observe means to notice or measure carefully so evidence can be recorded.

Visual: The everyday word look is refined into a science glossary card with evidence and record icons.

guided_practice

discipline-context-dock

Sort each vocabulary card into the subject context where it is being used most precisely.

Practise using sentence clues and disciplinary purpose rather than topic association alone.

Audio: Read the whole sentence. Ask what work the word is doing in this subject.

Visual: Sentence cards dock into English, science, history, geography and mathematics boards.

checkpoint

visual-glossary-build

Build a visual glossary card with meaning, word parts, subject context and one accurate sentence.

Check that learners can consolidate vocabulary knowledge beyond matching a definition.

Audio: A useful glossary card shows what the word means, how it is built, where it belongs and how it works in a sentence.

Visual: A glossary card fills with definition, morphemes, context icon, example and non-example.

teach_back

companion-register-repair

Teach Lyra why using a long word is not the same as using a precise word.

Repair register and precision misconceptions through explanation.

Audio: I chose the longest word because it sounded clever. What should I check before using it?

Visual: The companion compares a vague impressive sentence with a precise subject-fit sentence.

Question Variants

en-y7-disciplinary-vocabulary-q-morph-predict

morphology-builder review difficulty 5

Use the word parts to predict the meaning of 'reconstruct' in this history sentence: 'Historians reconstruct the events from letters and records.'

Reconstruct combines re- meaning again with construct meaning build; in history, it means piecing together events again from evidence.

en-y7-disciplinary-vocabulary-q-evidence-context

discipline-context-sort review difficulty 6

Match the word 'evidence' to the subject meaning used in each sentence.

Evidence has a shared idea of support, but the precise form changes by subject: textual detail in English and measured observations in science.

en-y7-disciplinary-vocabulary-q-glossary-card

visual-glossary-card review difficulty 5

Complete a visual glossary card for the geography word 'erosion'.

The completed card connects erosion to wearing away, the related word erode, a coastal visual anchor, a correct geography sentence and a contrasting non-example.

en-y7-disciplinary-vocabulary-q-precision-register

precision-choice review difficulty 4

Choose the most precise word for this science sentence: 'The beaker was heated until the liquid began to ____.'

Evaporate is the precise science vocabulary for a liquid changing into a gas; the other choices are vague or too informal.

en-y7-disciplinary-vocabulary-q-discipline-sort

discipline-context-sort review difficulty 7

Sort the word 'source' by the subject meaning in each sentence.

Source changes its precise meaning with the discipline: a historical document, the beginning of a river, or a text used as evidence.

morpheme-meaning-builds

morphology-builder developing 480 planned

Use prefixes, roots and suffixes to make a meaning prediction and identify likely word class.

Use high-utility KS3 word parts such as re-, inter-, sub-, -tion, -ity, graph, struct, port and spect; avoid false certainty where etymology is opaque.

Distinguish everyday and disciplinary meanings using sentence and subject clues.

Include words such as evidence, source, table, volume, current, function, character and observe with safe, supplied contexts.

discipline-purpose-sorters

discipline-context-sort secure 420 planned

Sort vocabulary by how a discipline uses the word, not merely by a related topic.

Use short, subject-authentic but self-contained sentences; no item should require prior specialist knowledge beyond the supplied text.

precision-register-choices

Choose vocabulary that fits register, meaning and sentence grammar rather than the longest or most dramatic word.

Avoid penalising dialect or home language; focus on subject precision and Standard English requirements for school writing.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use calm card movement, matte backgrounds, no countdowns and no flashing correct/incorrect effects.

reduced_motion

Replace tile splitting, docking and register sliders with static labelled panels and step-by-step reveals.

audio_first

Read the target word, morpheme parts, context sentence and answer choices aloud, with replay for glossary cards.

reading_support

Use line focus, adjustable spacing, syllable and morpheme chunking, glossary popovers and short subject-context extracts.

attention_support

Present one decision at a time: word parts, context clues, subject sort, precision check, then example sentence.

processing_support

Allow card rehearsal, spoken planning, morpheme reference cards and extra wait time before sentence-fit answers.

confidence_support

Credit a correct clue or morpheme even when the final meaning needs repair, then offer one focused next step.

eal_support

Provide visual glossary cards, cognate notes where reliable, spoken models and explicit everyday-versus-technical comparisons.

switch_access

All sorters and glossary builders have linear scan order, confirm/cancel controls and no drag-only path.

simple_text

Every visual glossary, morphology and sorter task has a plain text version with the same answer model.
