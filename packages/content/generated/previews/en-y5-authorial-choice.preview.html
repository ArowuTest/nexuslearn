Teaching Journey

warm_up

single-choice-contrast

Compare 'The door opened' with 'The door crept open'. What changes when the writer chooses crept?

Activate precise word comparison before naming techniques.

Audio: Notice the exact changed word, then say what it suggests in this sentence.

Visual: Two sentence strips differ in one highlighted word.

concept_launch

choice-effect-chain-build

Build a chain: exact choice, meaning in context, possible reader response.

Establish an evidence-led model that separates textual observation from interpretation.

Audio: Start with what is actually written. Explain what it suggests here, then use may, might or encourages for the reader response.

Visual: Three linked panels labelled CHOICE, MEANING and POSSIBLE EFFECT.

worked_example

connotation-web-trace

The storm 'clawed at the station walls'. Trace how clawed shapes the scene.

Model contextual connotation, personification and bounded effect language.

Audio: Clawed suggests repeated animal-like attack. This makes the storm seem hostile and may make the station feel vulnerable.

Visual: The word clawed connects to repeated attack, hostile storm and vulnerable station.

guided_practice

controlled-rewrite-compare

Replace one choice, reread both versions and decide which better fits the writer's purpose.

Evaluate effect through controlled comparison rather than technique spotting.

Audio: Keep the event the same. Change one word, sentence pattern or viewpoint choice, then compare what each version foregrounds.

Visual: A split editor aligns original and alternative with an evidence note beneath each.

checkpoint

evidence-beam-check

Which explanation is stronger: 'It makes you read on' or one that names what the words suggest and why that matters here?

Detect generic, unsupported reader-effect statements.

Audio: A strong explanation can point back to exact language and the situation in the text.

Visual: An evidence beam reaches the precise explanation but not the generic claim.

teach_back

evidence-chain-replay

Teach your companion which choice matters most, what it suggests and one reasonable limit on your claim.

Gather independent evaluation, evidence selection and epistemic caution.

Audio: Quote briefly, explain the contextual meaning and say the response the choice may encourage.

Visual: A quote, interpretation and possible-effect card remain visible during explanation.

Question Variants

en-y5-authorial-choice-q-crept

phrase-highlight review difficulty 4

Read: 'The door crept open, although nobody stood beside it.' Which choice most strongly makes the door seem as if it is moving secretly?

Crept suggests slow, quiet and secret movement, making the door seem unsettlingly alive in this context.

en-y5-authorial-choice-q-storm-claws

evidence-explain review difficulty 6

The writer says, 'The storm clawed at the station walls.' Which explanation best follows the evidence?

The animal-like verb suggests repeated attack. That interpretation supports a hostile mood and a possible sense that the station is exposed.

en-y5-authorial-choice-q-pace

reader-effect-choice review difficulty 7

A character discovers a warning light. Which version best creates a sudden stop after a flowing approach?

The longer approach establishes steady movement, then the short sentence foregrounds the sudden red warning.

en-y5-authorial-choice-q-viewpoint

evidence-explain review difficulty 7

The narrator stays with Ivo, who cannot see what is inside the sealed pod. What effect may this viewpoint choice encourage?

Restricting information to Ivo's experience can make the reader share his uncertainty, while the evidence does not prove what is inside.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm Orbit City reading console with static text, one evidence link at a time and no ambient animation behind passages.

reduced_motion

Replace evidence beams, rhythm pulses and viewpoint masks with underlines, numbered links and before-and-after still panels.

audio_first

Provide human-reviewed passage narration with sentence and paragraph replay; preserve the printed quotation and never use automatic prosody as evidence of authorial effect.

reading_support

Offer line focus, adjustable spacing, glossary-in-context, short extract mode and a phrase list that mirrors text highlighting.

attention_support

Keep the routine visible as CHOICE-MEANING-POSSIBLE EFFECT-CHECK and chunk longer extracts before comparison.

processing_support

Remove timers, allow repeated reading, keep original and alternative versions visible and accept oral, typed or structured evidence chains.

confidence_support

Credit accurate noticing before interpretation, label responses as evidence-based possibilities and allow revision without losing a valid quotation.

eal_support

Preteach key literal meanings and cultural references, distinguish dictionary meaning from contextual suggestion and accept clear evidence-based explanations in accessible language.
