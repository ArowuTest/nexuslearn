Teaching Journey

warm_up

evidence-assumption-sort

Circle what the representation tells you exactly and mark what you must not assume.

Activate attention to labels, tick marks, hidden edges, axes, scales and units.

Audio: Use stated marks and values as evidence. A picture alone may not be drawn to scale.

Visual: Angle, polygon, cube drawing and line graph each show evidence and assumption columns.

concept_launch

reasoning-console-build

Read the representation, model the relationship and justify your conclusion with a property, scale or unit.

Introduce one transferable reasoning routine across geometry and statistics.

Audio: First read the conventions. Next show the relationship. Finally state the evidence for your answer.

Visual: A three-panel READ-MODEL-JUSTIFY console links diagrams, calculations and explanation stems.

worked_example

total-and-difference-align

Find a missing angle on a straight line, then find the change between two points on a line graph.

Model additive structure in two representations while keeping degrees and data units distinct.

Audio: Angles on a straight line total 180 degrees. Graph change is the difference between two correctly read values.

Visual: A 180-degree bar and a graph difference bar appear side by side with their own units.

guided_practice

station-evidence-sync

Rotate through angle, shape, graph and timetable stations. Record the evidence before the answer.

Coordinate convention reading, property checking, scale interpretation and calculation without mixing domains.

Audio: At every station, name the evidence and unit before you submit.

Visual: Four accessible station tabs preserve a common evidence-reason-answer table.

checkpoint

visual-guess-freeze

The drawing looks regular and the graph point looks like 17. What must you check before agreeing?

Detect visual guessing from unmarked shapes and unexamined graph intervals.

Audio: Check equality marks for the polygon and calculate the value of one graph interval.

Visual: A regular-looking unmarked polygon and a non-unit graph scale receive evidence warning flags.

teach_back

dual-rule-replay

Teach your companion one rule for angles or shapes and one rule for graphs or timetables.

Gather explanation evidence across both curriculum areas.

Audio: Give an example, name the convention and explain one mistake the rule prevents.

Visual: The learner selects two evidence cards and records a spoken or typed explanation.

Question Variants

ma-y5-geometry-and-statistics-q-straight-line

angle-workbench review difficulty 5

Two adjacent angles make a straight line. One angle is 128 degrees. Find the other angle.

180 - 128 = 52, so the missing angle is 52 degrees.

ma-y5-geometry-and-statistics-q-regular-polygon

shape-evidence-map review difficulty 5

A pentagon has five equal sides, but its angles are not all equal. Is it regular?

The pentagon is irregular because regular polygons require all sides and all angles to be equal.

ma-y5-geometry-and-statistics-q-line-graph

graph-table-investigation review difficulty 5

A line graph records 18 litres on Monday and 32 litres on Thursday. How much greater is Thursday's amount?

Thursday's 32 litres is 14 litres greater than Monday's 18 litres.

ma-y5-geometry-and-statistics-q-timetable

graph-table-investigation review difficulty 7

A science show starts at 13:48 and ends at 14:35. How long does it last?

From 13:48 to 14:00 is 12 minutes, and from 14:00 to 14:35 is 35 minutes; 12 + 35 = 47 minutes.

angle-estimate-draw-calculate

angle-workbench expected 320 planned

Estimate, compare, draw and calculate angles at a point and on a straight line.

Vary orientation and arm length independently; accept sensible estimation ranges; do not imply diagrams are to scale unless explicitly stated.

polygon-regularity-evidence

shape-evidence-map expected 280 planned

Classify regular and irregular polygons from explicit side and angle evidence.

Include rotated, concave and familiar-looking distractors; never require classification from appearance where equality is not established.

solid-from-view-or-net

shape-evidence-map secure 280 planned

Identify possible 3-D shapes from 2-D views, nets and face-edge-vertex evidence.

Permit multiple possible solids when the view is insufficient; provide non-rotating labelled alternatives and verify every net geometrically.

line-graph-comparison-sum-difference

graph-table-investigation expected 340 planned

Read non-unit scales and solve comparison, sum and difference problems from line graphs.

Use honest axes, equal intervals and plausible data; avoid truncated-axis visual claims unless the task explicitly critiques them.

timetable-route-and-duration

graph-table-investigation stretch 280 planned

Read multi-row timetables and calculate waiting, journey and elapsed times across an hour.

Check chronological validity, 12/24-hour notation and accessible table headings; do not rely on culturally specific travel knowledge.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm Orbit City analysis desk with one representation at a time, muted backgrounds and no automatic spinning solids or moving graph traces.

reduced_motion

Replace ray rotation, solid turning and graph drawing with numbered static states, typed values and complete tables.

audio_first

Announce representation type, labels, equality marks, scale and units before reading the question; provide replay without revealing the answer.

reading_support

Use short prompts, symbol glossaries, line focus and a separate evidence list so visual-spatial reasoning is not hidden by dense text.

attention_support

Keep a READ MARKS-SHOW RELATIONSHIP-CALCULATE-JUSTIFY strip and hide unrelated station controls.

processing_support

Remove timers, allow typed or step-button construction, preserve annotations and provide graph-table and diagram-text toggles.

confidence_support

Credit accurate evidence reading before calculation, allow not-enough-information answers and frame visual traps as representation checks.

eal_support

Preteach regular, irregular, acute, obtuse, reflex, scale, interval, duration, face, edge and vertex with labelled examples and sentence frames.
