Teaching Journey

warm_up

feature-lens-scan

Look closely. Which features can you observe and use for grouping?

Focus learners on observable evidence before naming or classifying.

Audio: Scientists group living things using features they can observe carefully.

Visual: Local organism cards show zoomable features with no group labels at first.

concept_launch

multiple-valid-sorts

Living things can be grouped in more than one useful way.

Introduce classification as evidence-based grouping rather than one fixed classroom sort.

Audio: One sort might use backbone or no backbone. Another might use flowering or non-flowering plants. The feature must match the group.

Visual: The same cards are sorted by different valid feature rules.

worked_example

branching-key-path

Answer each yes or no question using the evidence on the card.

Model classification-key navigation as a sequence of observable decisions.

Audio: At each branch, answer only the question you can check. Follow the yes or no path.

Visual: A ladybird card moves through a simple key: animal, has legs, six legs, hard wing cases.

guided_practice

key-builder-branch-check

Build a simple key that separates four living things using clear yes or no questions.

Move from using keys to designing keys with unambiguous observable features.

Audio: Choose a question that splits the cards. Then choose the next question for the smaller group.

Visual: A drag-and-drop branching key builder checks whether every endpoint identifies one card.

checkpoint

habitat-before-after-evidence

The pond becomes shaded and has less plant growth. Which living things might be affected, and what evidence supports your idea?

Connect specific environmental changes to likely needs of organisms without overclaiming.

Audio: A habitat change can affect food, shelter, water or light. Use the evidence before deciding.

Visual: Before and after pond evidence cards show light, plants, insects and shelter indicators.

teach_back

companion-feature-repair

Teach your companion why 'I do not like it' is not a scientific classification feature.

Repair subjective grouping and promote respectful language about living things.

Audio: A useful classification feature is something others can observe and check.

Visual: A companion tries to group mini-beasts by scary/not scary, then revises to number of legs.

Question Variants

sc-y4-living-things-and-habitats-q-feature-sort

feature-sort review difficulty 4

Which grouping rule is scientific and checkable for these living things?

Having a shell is an observable feature. Friendly, nice and familiar are opinions or experiences, not reliable classification features.

sc-y4-living-things-and-habitats-q-follow-key

classification-key review difficulty 5

Use the key to identify the organism: animal; has legs; has six legs; has hard wing cases.

The card follows the animal, legs, six legs and hard wing cases path, which identifies it as a ladybird in this key.

sc-y4-living-things-and-habitats-q-key-question

key-builder review difficulty 6

You need a yes/no question to separate a snail from a spider, beetle and bee. Which question is clearest?

A shell is an observable feature that separates the snail from the other shown organisms. The other questions are unclear or opinion-based.

sc-y4-living-things-and-habitats-q-pond-change

habitat-change-evidence review difficulty 6

A pond has much less plant growth after nearby trees make it very shaded. Which evidence-supported effect is most likely?

Less plant growth could reduce food or shelter for some pond animals. The evidence supports a possible effect, not a certain immediate outcome for every animal.

sc-y4-living-things-and-habitats-q-bee-not-pest

evidence-explain review difficulty 5

A learner says, 'Bees are pests, so they should be in a bad animals group.' Which scientific response is best?

Scientific classification uses observable features, not labels such as bad or scary. Six legs and wings are checkable evidence.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use calm habitat scenes, no sudden animal movement and no distressing injury, death or predator imagery.

reduced_motion

Use static organism cards, step-by-step key highlighting and before/after habitat panels instead of animated movement.

audio_first

Narrate organism features and key questions with replay; do not require identifying species from audio.

reading_support

Pair vocabulary with icons, use yes/no buttons, provide sentence frames and keep organism names decodable where possible.

attention_support

Show only the current key branch or habitat evidence panel; hide unused cards until needed.

processing_support

Allow zoom, pause and backtracking in keys; preserve previous answers so learners can revise without restarting.

confidence_support

Treat reclassification as improving the key, not being wrong; validate careful observation before correction.

eal_support

Preteach classify, feature, key, yes, no, habitat, environment, change, food, shelter, water and light with paired images.
