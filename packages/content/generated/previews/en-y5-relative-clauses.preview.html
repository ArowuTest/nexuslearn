Teaching Journey

warm_up

antecedent-arrow-trace

Underline the noun being described and draw an arrow from the extra information back to it.

Activate noun reference before naming relative-clause forms.

Audio: Ask which person, thing, place or time the extra clause describes.

Visual: Noun phrase and clause cards are joined by a selectable antecedent arrow.

concept_launch

relative-link-build

Attach useful information to a noun using a relative link that matches the meaning.

Frame relative clauses as purposeful noun expansion and reference.

Audio: The link may show a person, thing, place, time or possession, and sometimes the pronoun can be implied.

Visual: Meaning-labelled links connect noun cards to complete clause cards.

worked_example

embedded-clause-dock

The observatory, which stands above Orbit City, tracks distant storms.

Model an embedded relative clause, clear antecedent and paired punctuation.

Audio: Which points back to the observatory. The clause is inserted after that noun, and the main sentence remains complete around it.

Visual: The main clause forms a complete track while the relative clause docks beside observatory.

guided_practice

sentence-combiner-sync

Combine two related sentences, place the clause beside its noun, choose the link and reread for meaning and punctuation.

Coordinate sentence combining, antecedent proximity, relative-word choice and fluency.

Audio: Keep the intended noun close, remove repeated words and check that the main clause still works.

Visual: A COMBINE-PLACE-LINK-REREAD editor preserves both source meanings.

checkpoint

relative-test-contrast

In 'I know that the door is locked', does that point back to a noun? Compare it with 'the door that is locked'.

Distinguish relative that from other grammatical uses by testing noun reference.

Audio: A relative word introduces a clause that describes a noun. Not every that does this job.

Visual: Only the relative example produces a valid arrow to door.

teach_back

clause-choice-replay

Teach your companion why you chose the relative link, clause position and punctuation.

Gather metalinguistic evidence and reader-effect reasoning.

Audio: Name the antecedent, explain the meaning link and show that the sentence remains clear.

Visual: A reference arrow, meaning icon and sentence read-through remain visible during explanation.

Question Variants

en-y5-relative-clauses-q-who-link

clause-link-map review difficulty 4

Choose the clearest relative word: 'The engineer ___ repaired the rover explained the fault.'

'Who repaired the rover' is a relative clause describing the engineer.

en-y5-relative-clauses-q-placement

sentence-combiner review difficulty 5

Combine the sentences so the relative clause clearly describes the drone. 'The drone entered the tunnel. The drone carried the sensor.'

Placing 'which carried the sensor' directly after 'the drone' makes the intended antecedent clear.

en-y5-relative-clauses-q-that-test

clause-link-map review difficulty 6

Which sentence uses 'that' to begin a relative clause describing a noun?

In the first sentence, 'that arrived at noon' describes 'the message', so it is a relative clause.

en-y5-relative-clauses-q-punctuation

relative-clause-editor review difficulty 6

Punctuate the embedded relative clause: 'The observatory which stands above Orbit City tracks distant storms.'

The paired commas mark the embedded additional information, while 'The observatory tracks distant storms' remains the main clause.

antecedent-and-relative-link

clause-link-map developing 320 planned

Identify the antecedent and choose a relative link that accurately expresses person, thing, place, time or possession.

Use inclusive names and roles, avoid simplistic who-for-people rules where whose or that is also possible, and accept contextually valid alternatives.

Allow more than one fluent combination when reference and meaning remain clear; do not reward syntactic complexity by itself.

Revise relative clauses for precision, fluency and useful detail rather than adding complexity mechanically.

Preserve author voice, permit concise main-clause alternatives and ensure style judgements have a clear communicative purpose.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm Orbit City writing desk with static clause cards, one reference arrow at a time and no decorative text movement.

reduced_motion

Replace card docking and arrow drawing with numbered noun-clause pairs, persistent brackets and before/after sentence panels.

audio_first

Read source sentences separately, pause around the relative clause and replay the combined sentence neutrally without exaggerating the answer.

reading_support

Use clause chunking, adjustable spacing, antecedent highlighting with text labels, vocabulary glosses and a plain linear editor.

attention_support

Use a fixed FIND NOUN-CHOOSE LINK-PLACE CLAUSE-REREAD sequence and reveal one decision at a time.

processing_support

Remove timers, preserve source sentences, allow oral rehearsal, undo and structured-choice explanations before free writing.

confidence_support

Credit clear simple sentences, treat clause removal as a valid revision and preserve every correct part during targeted repair.

eal_support

Preteach person, thing, place, time and possession meanings with paired examples; compare relative patterns with learners' home languages where helpful without assuming direct equivalence.
