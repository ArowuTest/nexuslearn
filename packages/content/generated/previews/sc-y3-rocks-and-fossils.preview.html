I can compare rocks, explain a simple fossil sequence and describe what soil contains.

Observe, test, compare and group rocks by simple properties; explain how some fossils form in rock over long periods; and recognise soil as a mixture containing rock particles and organic matter.

Teaching Journey

warm_up

observe-test-card-sort

Which observations describe what a material looks like, and which require a fair test?

Reactivate the distinction between observable features and tested properties.

Audio: We can observe colour and grain size directly. We need a planned test for properties such as water absorption.

Visual: Cards for colour, grain, scratch result and absorbed drops sort into OBSERVE and TEST columns.

concept_launch

field-sample-tray-open

Inspect four rock samples and decide which evidence could be used to group them.

Frame classification as a decision based on stated properties rather than one correct naming system.

Audio: Scientists can group the same samples in different useful ways if they state and apply one rule consistently.

Visual: Four varied samples sit beside magnifier, scratch and drop-test tools.

worked_example

equal-drop-absorption-test

Compare water absorption using the same number of drops, waiting time and sample area.

Model a simple comparative test and precise property language.

Audio: Change the rock sample, keep the drop amount and waiting time the same, then compare the evidence.

Visual: Two samples receive five equal drops and a timer before the remaining water is recorded.

guided_practice

fossil-layer-sequence

Order the cards from a living organism to burial, more layers, rock formation and later discovery.

Build an age-appropriate causal sequence while avoiding instant-fossil and whole-organism models.

Audio: Some remains or traces are buried by sediment. Over a very long time, layers build and harden, and preserved evidence may remain.

Visual: A five-panel sequence shows a shell, burial, layered sediment, rock with an impression and exposed fossil.

checkpoint

soil-components-reveal

A soil sample contains grains, leaf pieces, water and air spaces. Which claim about soil is best supported?

Detect single-substance soil models and connect observations to mixture language.

Audio: Name the different parts you can observe, then decide whether soil is one substance or a mixture.

Visual: A magnified soil cross-section has text-labelled mineral grains, organic fragments, water and air spaces.

teach_back

geology-evidence-lock

Teach your companion how rock evidence, fossil layers and soil components tell three different scientific stories.

Gather explanation evidence and keep observation, process and composition claims distinct.

Audio: For each claim, point to the observation, test result or sequence step that supports it.

Visual: Three evidence frames are labelled COMPARE, SEQUENCE and MIXTURE.

Question Variants

sc-y3-rocks-and-fossils-q-group-by-grain

classification-rule review difficulty 3

Group the samples using grain size. Which two belong together under the rule 'large visible grains'?

Samples A and C both have large visible grains. Their different colours do not change this grouping rule.

sc-y3-rocks-and-fossils-q-absorption-test

rock-property-lab review difficulty 4

Each sample receives five equal drops for the same time. Sample P absorbs four drops and sample Q absorbs one. Which conclusion is supported?

Under the same conditions, P took in four drops and Q took in one, so P absorbed more water.

sc-y3-rocks-and-fossils-q-shell-sequence

fossil-sequence review difficulty 5

Put the fossil-formation events in the most sensible order.

Burial comes before layers build and harden over a very long time; later erosion may expose the preserved evidence.

sc-y3-rocks-and-fossils-q-soil-mixture

soil-mixture-model review difficulty 4

A soil sample contains mineral grains, small decayed leaf pieces, water and air spaces. Which description is most accurate?

The sample contains different materials, including mineral rock particles and organic matter, so soil is a mixture.

rock-observation-and-grouping

classification-rule intro 300 planned

Describe and group varied rock samples using explicit observable properties.

Avoid implying that images identify rock type with certainty; allow multiple valid groups when the rule is stated and consistently applied.

comparative-rock-property-tests

rock-property-lab developing 320 planned

Compare hardness and water absorption through simple controlled virtual tests.

State that classroom scratch tests compare samples rather than assign absolute scientific hardness; verify all generated results and conclusions match.

fossil-formation-sequences

fossil-sequence expected 340 planned

Order and explain simple pathways by which remains, impressions or traces can be preserved in rock.

Use 'some' and 'may' accurately, represent long time without a precise invented duration and distinguish original remains, impressions and traces.

soil-components-and-comparison

soil-mixture-model secure 300 planned

Recognise soils as mixtures and compare samples using evidence about rock particles and organic matter.

Do not present one fixed recipe for all soils; distinguish organic matter from living organisms and acknowledge air and water without exceeding the objective.

rocks-fossils-soils-retrieval

evidence-explain retrieval 260 planned

Retain property, fossil-process and soil-mixture understanding through short evidence-linked review.

Keep the scientific focus explicit, use non-visual alternatives and avoid unsafe instructions involving unknown dust, sharp samples or outdoor collection.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm field-lab background, one sample or layer sequence at a time, no scraping sound effects and no dusty particle animations.

reduced_motion

Replace falling sediment, settling jars and erosion animations with numbered static layers and labelled before/after panels.

audio_first

Describe each sample's observable properties, announce test conditions and results, and narrate fossil events in chronological order with replay.

reading_support

Pair property terms with icons and concise definitions, keep observation tables visible and use one causal claim per sentence.

attention_support

Use fixed OBSERVE-TEST-RECORD-COMPARE and ORGANISM-BURY-LAYERS-ROCK-EXPOSE routines, changing one variable at a time.

processing_support

Remove timers, allow step-by-step sequences, preserve test data and provide list, table and text alternatives to spatial grouping.

confidence_support

Treat grouping rules and predictions as testable proposals, retain accurate observations during repair and distinguish an unsupported claim from a failed learner.

eal_support

Preteach rock, grain, property, absorb, sediment, fossil, soil, mixture and organic matter with real-object photographs, gestures and sentence frames.
