Teaching Journey

warm_up

source-reflector-sort

Sort each item: does it make its own light, or can it only be seen when light reflects from it?

Distinguish sources from illuminated or reflective objects before modelling vision.

Audio: The Sun and a switched-on lamp are sources. The Moon and a road sign reflect light.

Visual: Item cards move into MAKES LIGHT and REFLECTS LIGHT columns with explanation icons.

concept_launch

source-object-eye-path

In a dark cave, switch on a lamp and trace the path that lets the explorer see the map.

Build a source-to-object-to-eye model and define darkness as insufficient light.

Audio: Light travels from the lamp to the map, reflects from the map and enters the eye.

Visual: Numbered arrows connect lamp, map and eye; darkness is shown by absent arrows rather than black material.

worked_example

surface-reflection-test

Compare foil, white card and dark fabric. All reflect some light, but the amount and direction we observe are different.

Prevent mirror-only reflection and connect claims to observed brightness.

Audio: A mirror gives a clear directed reflection, but many ordinary surfaces also reflect light into our eyes.

Visual: A virtual torch illuminates three surfaces while a sensor records relative reflected-light bars.

guided_practice

shadow-distance-lab

Move one part of the source-object-screen setup, measure the shadow and record what changed.

Investigate shadow formation and size through one-variable comparisons.

Audio: Keep two positions the same. Change one distance, measure the shadow and describe the pattern.

Visual: A safe virtual lamp, opaque shape, screen, ruler and results table share a fixed baseline.

checkpoint

block-or-transmit-compare

A transparent sheet and an opaque card are placed in the light path. Which makes the clearer shadow, and why?

Check that shadow explanation uses blocked light, material behaviour and a source.

Audio: A clearer shadow forms when more light is blocked from reaching the screen.

Visual: Side-by-side light paths show transmission through transparent material and blocking by opaque card.

teach_back

sun-safety-shield

Teach your companion how to investigate sunlight safely without ever looking at the Sun.

Gather safety reasoning and transfer light knowledge to responsible observation.

Audio: Use shadows, reflected-light observations or adult-approved instruments. Never look directly at the Sun, even through a camera, lens or dark material.

Visual: Safe choices receive a shield icon; direct viewing and improvised filters remain disabled and clearly labelled unsafe.

Question Variants

sc-y3-light-q-see-map

light-path-model review difficulty 3

Put the light path in order so the explorer can see the map.

Light travels from the lamp to the map, reflects from the map and then enters the eye.

sc-y3-light-q-reflection-surfaces

reflection-test review difficulty 6

A virtual torch shines equally on a mirror, white card and dark fabric. Which conclusion fits the observations?

Each surface reflects some light in the model, although the mirror gives a stronger directed reflection and the dark fabric gives a lower reading.

sc-y3-light-q-shadow-closer-source

shadow-investigation review difficulty 7

The opaque object and screen stay still. The lamp moves closer to the object. What happens to the shadow on the screen?

In this setup, moving the lamp closer makes the blocked region spread more widely by the time it reaches the screen, so the shadow becomes larger.

sc-y3-light-q-sun-safety

evidence-explain review difficulty 4

Which is a safe way to investigate sunlight?

Measuring a shadow allows a sunlight investigation without looking at the Sun. Direct viewing can damage eyes and is unsafe through cameras, lenses or improvised filters.

Explain seeing as light travelling from a source, reflecting from an object and entering the eye.

Use arrows as a simple model, not a claim that light moves only in single narrow rays; avoid anthropomorphic eye beams.

Distinguish light sources, reflectors and situations with too little light to see.

Do not imply all low-light conditions are complete darkness; language should distinguish no light from not enough light for the task.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use a calm dark-grey lab rather than pure black, no flashing sources, no sudden brightness changes and no ambient hum by default.

reduced_motion

Replace travelling light arrows and changing shadows with numbered static path panels and labelled before/after measurements.

audio_first

Narrate source state, path order, material and observation; offer replay without using spatial language alone.

reading_support

Pair each scientific term with a diagram and short definition, keep one claim per sentence and leave observation tables visible during explanation.

attention_support

Use fixed SOURCE-OBJECT-EYE and SOURCE-BLOCKER-SCREEN routines, changing only one variable per investigation.

processing_support

Remove timers, allow pause and step controls, preserve measurements, and provide side-by-side comparisons and data-table alternatives.

confidence_support

Treat predictions as testable ideas, retain correct parts of a model during repair and do not reward speed or public performance.

eal_support

Preteach source, reflect, eye, opaque, transparent, block, shadow and safety with icons, gestures and sentence frames.
