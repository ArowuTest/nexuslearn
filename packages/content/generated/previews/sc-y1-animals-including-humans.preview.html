Teaching Journey

warm_up

animal-feature-soft-focus

Tap one body part you can see.

Establish careful observation before naming or grouping an animal.

Audio: Look closely. What body part can you see?

Visual: One large, uncluttered animal illustration with generous feature hotspots.

concept_launch

animal-family-cards-gather

These animals look different, but they all belong to the animal family.

Broaden the animal concept to include humans and varied animal groups.

Audio: A fish, a bird, a frog and a human are all animals.

Visual: Five cards enter one broad animal-family frame without ranking or competition.

worked_example

whale-fish-feature-compare

A whale lives in water, but it is a mammal, not a fish.

Model grouping from several relevant features rather than habitat alone.

Audio: Where an animal lives is one clue, but it is not the whole answer. A whale is a mammal.

Visual: Whale and fish comparison cards with fins, body shape and group badges; no anatomy beyond Year 1 need.

guided_practice

animal-card-group-snap

Sort the animals, then tap a body feature that helped you.

Practise grouping common animals and justifying choices with observable structure.

Audio: Choose a group, then show the feature you noticed.

Visual: Large animal cards, audio-labelled group mats and optional feature hotspots.

checkpoint

sense-path-ear-highlight

Which body part helps you hear the bell?

Check links between basic human body parts and senses in a familiar safe context.

Audio: Think about the body part you use to notice a sound.

Visual: A child outline with eyes, ears and nose as large choices; bell shown as a context cue.

teach_back

companion-human-mammal-link

Teach Pip why a human belongs in the animal family.

Repair the human-versus-animal misconception through age-appropriate comparison.

Audio: I thought humans were not animals. What can we observe that helps us understand?

Visual: Human and other mammal cards with shared body-part icons and a mammal group frame.

picture-sort review difficulty 4

A whale lives in water. Which animal group does it belong to?

A whale is a mammal even though it lives in water. Habitat alone does not decide an animal group.

sc-y1-animals-including-humans-q-bird-feature

feature-tap review difficulty 3

Tap a body covering that helps you identify the robin as a bird.

Feathers are an observable body covering that identifies the robin as a bird.

Humans are animals, and the animal group humans belong to is mammals.

Identify familiar animals and notice one clear visible body feature before grouping.

Use anatomically clear, child-friendly images; avoid frightening poses, injury, predation or misleading cartoon anatomy.

Adaptive Support

low_sensory

Use still animal images, optional environmental sound, plain backgrounds and no sudden animal movement or loud calls.

reduced_motion

Replace card travel, scanner sweeps and sense paths with static outlines and instant selected states.

audio_first

Read animal names, group labels, prompts and explanations aloud with replay and visible playback controls.

reading_support

Use clear pictures, body icons and spoken labels; written text remains short and is never the only source of a clue.

attention_support

Show one animal or comparison pair at a time and limit early choices to three.

processing_support

Allow repeated observation, audio replay, extra response time and a feature-by-feature reveal.

confidence_support

Praise accurate observations even when the group choice needs repair, then invite a second look without red failure states.

eal_support

Preteach animal, human, body, fish, bird, mammal, eye, ear, nose and sense using pictures and gestures.
